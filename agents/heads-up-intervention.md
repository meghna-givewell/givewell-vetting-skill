## Intervention-Specific Plausibility Calibration

**Bidirectional magnitude check — applies to all checks below**: For every adjustment or parameter labeled "Guess," "estimate," or "assumption" with no external source, check the magnitude in *both directions* — not only whether it is implausibly high (too optimistic) but also whether it is implausibly low (too conservative). A conservative-looking value is not automatically correct: an understated downward adjustment means CE is overstated, but an overstated downward adjustment means CE is understated and the program may appear less cost-effective than it is. Both directions affect funding allocation.

Common below-range patterns to catch:
- **FP commodity programs — expiry/waste**: Rates below 5% for bulk commodity distribution in SSA public-sector chains are unusually optimistic. Published estimates for comparable programs range 5–20% depending on supply chain maturity, cold-chain requirements, and method mix. A stated guess of -5% sits at the low edge of this range and warrants a flag even when the direction is conservative.
- **Supply chain loss adjustments**: Values below 5% for commodity programs lacking documented warehouse management controls or independent audit evidence are below the typical published range (USAID/DELIVER, PIC, Guttmacher) and should be flagged.

When a "Guess" value is at the low end of the plausible range, flag at the same severity as a value at the high end: Low/H if the value is plausible but undocumented, Medium/H if it falls outside the typical literature range. Run a targeted WebSearch (e.g., `"[commodity type] expiry rate supply chain [region]"`) before filing — if published estimates bracket the model value, note that as supporting evidence; if they don't, cite the contradicting range in the finding.

**Benefit completeness — applies to all intervention types**: Before running intervention-specific checks, review the grant document and literature context for program benefits that are *absent from the model entirely*. Ask: does the intervention have known secondary outcomes — mental health effects, economic spillovers, co-benefits to household members, or other pathways — mentioned in the grant doc or external literature that are not modeled? A model that omits a real benefit understates CE just as surely as one with a formula error. File as Low/H if an unmodeled benefit is plausible and non-trivial, noting that CE is likely understated and the researcher should confirm whether the omission is intentional. Do not file if the omission is documented (e.g., a cell note explaining why the component was excluded). Common examples: mental health benefits of breastfeeding programs, cognitive development effects of nutrition interventions, spillover vaccination effects in CCT programs.

Run the checks in this section only when the intervention type matches. These are grounded in GiveWell's own confirmed modeling errors from red teaming retrospectives (2023–2025).

**VAS — indirect deaths multiplier**: Verify the ratio of indirect deaths to direct deaths averted. For malaria programs (SMC, ITN), GiveWell uses 0.75 indirect deaths per direct death. VAS models have historically used a much higher ratio (~5 indirect per direct), which was flagged in GiveWell's 2025 red teaming as potentially implausible. Flag as Medium/H if the VAS model's indirect deaths multiplier substantially exceeds 1.0 without a documented basis distinguishing VAS from the malaria standard.

**VAS — evidence age and VAD prevalence**: (a) Verify the VAS mortality effect size is explicitly described as derived from 1980s–1990s RCTs and that the internal validity adjustment (−25%) and external validity adjustment (−41% to −79%) are both applied. If the model uses a raw 24% reduction without both adjustments, flag as High/D. (b) For Nigeria specifically, verify the vitamin A deficiency (VAD) prevalence is not 21.6% (an outdated estimate); the current recommended value is approximately 13.5% for 6–59 month olds, a reduction that materially lowers cost-effectiveness. Flag as High/D if the 21.6% figure is used without noting the update.

**SMC — RCT vs. observational gap**: When an SMC model uses an effect size derived from RCTs (typically ~75–79% malaria case reduction), verify that the researcher has explicitly acknowledged that large-scale observational studies of SMC programs find substantially smaller effects — roughly 2–3× smaller in some analyses. If the model uses the RCT estimate without any note about the observational gap or without an explicit downward adjustment, flag as Low/O (readability gap) or Medium/H if the observational estimate would materially change the CE assessment.

**SMC — cycles/transmission parameter linkage**: When a model has both a "number of SMC cycles" parameter and a "proportion of annual malaria transmission occurring during SMC delivery" parameter, verify both are updated together when either is changed. A documented error in GiveWell's Uganda SMC model: the cycles count was updated but the transmission proportion was not, causing ~15% underestimation of cost-effectiveness. If these two parameters appear independently hardcoded in different rows, flag as Medium/H and ask the researcher to confirm they are consistent.

**SMC — non-Sahel efficacy and seasonality parameters**: When an SMC model covers non-Sahel geographies (Uganda, Mozambique, DRC, Tanzania, etc.), verify that Sahel-derived parameters have not been applied without adjustment. Key benchmarks: (a) Sahel efficacy: ~79% malaria case reduction during season; non-Sahel efficacy: ~60–64% (reduced by SP resistance and different parasite/vector dynamics); (b) proportion of annual malaria deaths occurring during SMC season: ~70% in Sahel 4-cycle areas, ~52–62% in non-Sahel 5-cycle programs; (c) cost per child per season: ~$5–8 in Sahel, ~$10–11 for non-Sahel 5-cycle programs. If a non-Sahel model uses Sahel efficacy (79%) or seasonal concentration (~70%) without documented justification, flag as High/D — applying Sahel parameters in a non-Sahel setting materially overstates cost-effectiveness. Reference values: GiveWell's 2025 MC SMC Uganda (Karamoja) grant uses 64% efficacy, 62% seasonal concentration, $10.59/child for a 5-cycle program; GiveWell's 2024 MC SMC Mozambique grant uses 60% efficacy, citing SP resistance, yielding ~9x vs. 15–25x for Sahel programs.

**ITN — PBO net durability and chemical decay**: When an ITN model covers PBO (pyrethroid/piperonyl butoxide) nets, verify that (a) net durability is not assumed to be the same as older pyrethroid-only nets — expert analysis suggests median PBO net retention of ~1.6 years (vs. ~2 years for standard pyrethroid nets); and (b) PBO chemical decay is modeled — standard adjustments are approximately −20% effectiveness in year 2 and −40% in year 3. If the model uses a single undecayed effectiveness value across all years without decay adjustment, flag as Medium/H.

**Deworming — single-study income effect**: When a deworming model's primary benefit is long-term income effects, verify that the researcher acknowledges the income evidence derives almost entirely from one study (Miguel & Kremer 2004, Baird et al. 2012/2015) conducted in western Kenya with unusually heavy worm-infection intensity. Short-term health evidence (weight gain, cognition, hemoglobin) shows little to no effect in Cochrane meta-analyses. Flag as Low/O if the cell notes do not acknowledge the single-study provenance and the representativeness concern for modern, lower-burden programs.

**Malaria vaccines — 3-dose rebound**: When a malaria vaccine model uses the 3-dose schedule, verify that the model accounts for the severe malaria rebound observed in the Phase 3 trial: 3-dose VE against severe malaria was −44% in months 21–32 and −57% in months 33–end (worse than unvaccinated). If the model projects vaccine benefits across a multi-year horizon using the early-period 3-dose efficacy without accounting for the rebound, flag as High/D. Note: 4-dose VE against severe malaria was 32% overall with no observed rebound.

**Malaria vaccines — year-1 vs. multi-year efficacy horizon**: When a malaria vaccine model projects benefits across a multi-year horizon, verify that year-1 trial efficacy is not used as a flat multi-year estimate. Documented values: RTS,S 4-dose efficacy in the Phase 3 trial against clinical malaria is approximately 55–63% in year 1, declining to a 4-year blended average of approximately 31–36%. GiveWell's 2022 PATH RTS,S grant used 36.3% over a 4-year horizon. R21 shows higher year-1 efficacy (~75%) but multi-year data is limited. If a model uses year-1 efficacy across a modeling horizon longer than 12 months without decay adjustment or note explaining the choice, flag as High/D. This is distinct from the 3-dose rebound check: that check concerns a late-period negative rebound; this check concerns the general decay from year-1 to a multi-year average.

**Azithromycin aMDA — setting-specific generalizability and resistance offset**: When reviewing an azithromycin mass drug administration (aMDA) model, verify: (a) the MORDOR trial headline of 14% all-cause mortality reduction (aged 1–59 months) has not been applied without a setting-specific adjustment — MORDOR found near-null effects in Tanzania (lower-mortality setting) and ~18% in Niger (higher-mortality setting); applying 14% to a Tanzania-like setting overstates impact; (b) a downward adjustment for antibiotic resistance risk is included. Flag as High/D if the model uses a value above 20% for any setting — this exceeds the upper confidence bound of MORDOR. Flag as Medium/H if 14% is applied to a lower-mortality setting without a generalizability discount. Flag as Low/O if no antibiotic resistance offset is present.

**CMAM — case fatality rate vs. annual mortality rate**: When reviewing a CMAM (severe acute malnutrition treatment) model, verify two parameters are not confused: (a) the SAM case fatality rate (~20–30% of children who present with SAM will die without treatment) is different from the annual under-5 mortality risk in the target area (~6% per year is GiveWell's working estimate for program populations); using case fatality rate where annual mortality rate is needed, or vice versa, will materially mis-estimate the program's impact; flag as High/D if the two appear conflated; (b) additionality — if the model assumes 100% of children reached are "additionally" treated (i.e., would receive no treatment without the NGO), flag as Medium/H. Government and other programs treat some fraction of SAM cases; the correct input is the unmet-need fraction, not total SAM incidence. Recovery rate (~85–90% in outpatient CMAM) is also distinct from mortality reduction — flag if these are equated.

**CMAM — ceiling analysis GAM ICF formula**: When a CMAM model includes a ceiling analysis with a GAM (global acute malnutrition) ICF row that combines MAM and SAM ICFs, read that cell's formula in FORMULA mode. The expected formula is a simple prevalence-weighted average: `GAM_ICF = (MAM_prev × MAM_ICF + SAM_prev × SAM_ICF) / (MAM_prev + SAM_prev)`. If the formula instead weights by mortality ratios, Roodman RR values, or any factor other than raw prevalence shares, flag as Medium/D. Mortality-weighted averaging overstates the GAM ICF because SAM (higher ICF) receives more weight than its prevalence share implies — producing an inflated ICF, a more conservative ceiling, and a lower plausibility adjustment that reduces CE. Ask the researcher to confirm the intended weighting methodology and whether prevalence-only weighting is more appropriate. **CE impact guard**: Before quantifying the CE impact of a hardcoded ceiling parameter (cap value, maximum percentage), confirm via FORMULA mode that all formulas consuming that parameter — including the GAM ICF aggregation — are arithmetically correct. A CE impact estimate computed from a chain containing an undetected formula error will itself be wrong, sometimes in the opposite direction from the finding.

**HPV vaccination — advances-in-treatment discount**: When reviewing an HPV vaccination model, verify the "advances in cervical cancer prevention and treatment" discount (×0.70) is applied within the IV adjustment. This accounts for the fact that vaccinated girls today will face peak cervical cancer risk 30–50 years from now, by which time prevention and treatment will have improved materially. GiveWell's CHAI HPV BOTEC (December 2023) includes this as an explicit IV component — omitting it overstates cost-effectiveness by approximately 43%. Flag as Medium/H if absent. Additionally, for countries with cervical cancer age-standardized incidence above the African regional mean (~17.6 per 100,000), verify an "optimizer's curse" correction is applied — GiveWell uses 0.79 for Malawi (ASR 54.1 vs. mean 17.6); without this, CE is further overstated by ~27% for high-incidence countries. Also verify that 70% is used as the share of cervical cancer attributable to HPV16/18 (the targeted strains) — values above 75% should be flagged.

**IPTp — parity restriction on birthweight effect**: When reviewing an IPTp (intermittent preventive treatment in pregnancy) model that includes birthweight or low-birthweight (LBW) benefits, verify the effect is restricted to first pregnancies. The Cochrane evidence (Radeva-Petrova et al. 2014) shows: for first pregnancies — ~93g mean birthweight increase, 27% LBW reduction (RR 0.73); for all parities — essentially no birthweight benefit (mean difference −0.54g; RR for LBW 1.06, both non-significant). If the model applies the first-pregnancy effect to the full program population without adjusting for the proportion of first pregnancies, flag as High/D — this systematically overstates birthweight and downstream developmental benefits. Also flag if mortality benefits are presented as direct RCT evidence: IPTp trials were underpowered for mortality; mortality pathway is modeled indirectly.

**Water quality / chlorination — non-significant pooled RR and mandatory adjustments**: When reviewing a water quality or chlorination model: (a) verify the ~24% mortality reduction point estimate is not treated as a certain effect — GiveWell's meta-analysis of 5 chlorination RCTs gives pooled RR 0.88 (95% CI 0.69–1.13), which includes 1.0 and is not statistically significant; flag as Low/O if the CI is not acknowledged; (b) verify the ×0.75 internal validity adjustment for bundled interventions (those including safe storage or additional hygiene components beyond chlorination alone) is applied — omitting it overstates the adjusted effect by ~33%; flag as Medium/H if absent; (c) verify a leverage/funging adjustment is present — GiveWell's Uduma chlorination CEA applies −29% to −60% by country; flag as Medium/H if no leverage/funging treatment appears in a model for a third-party-implemented or co-financed program; (d) for models with an adherence adjustment tab using per-geography free chlorine residual (FCR) rates or similar monitoring-derived parameters: scan all FCR/residual chlorine rate cells across every geography column. Flag any value expressed as a round integer percentage (e.g., 52%) when other columns contain fractional values for the same parameter (e.g., 51.7%, 48.3%) — this asymmetry suggests the round value was manually entered rather than pulled from the source data, and may have been rounded incorrectly. Flag as Medium/H with Researcher judgment needed ✓ and recommend verifying against the original monitoring report or KSWTCS survey data. Also flag any FCR rate cell that lacks a source note regardless of whether it is rounded.

**New Incentives — standard adjustment set**: When reviewing a New Incentives (CCT for vaccination) CEA, verify the following standard adjustments are present; flag any that are missing without explanation:
- Internal validity downward adjustment (~−12%): other vaccine incentive RCTs find smaller effects
- Grantee-level factors adjustment (~−7%): uncertainty specific to NI's implementation
- Coverage trend adjustment: unvaccinated children declining ~2% per year (not 5% — updated post-red-team)
- Vaccine efficacy adjustment (~−14%): measles and BCG are less effective in Nigeria
- Self-report bias: −28% vs. BCG scar rate in RCT; ~+1% in scale-up settings
- Program exit / withdrawal effect (~−10% to −30% in new operating states): incentive withdrawal reduces vaccination rates 1–3 pp
- Fraud adjustment (~−12%: −10% double-enrollment + −2% other errors)

**TA grants — model type and structural consistency**: TA grant BOTECs follow one of three model types; identify which and verify the model structure matches:
- **Model 1** (accelerating introduction of a new technology — e.g. malaria vaccines, dual tests): coverage today = 0%; both "speed-up of introduction" and "speed-up of steady state" parameters should be present and non-zero.
- **Model 2** (improving coverage of an existing program — e.g. IFA, deworming): coverage today > 0%; "speed-up of introduction" should be 0 (program already exists); a separate "year TA begins to increase coverage" parameter should be present to model implementation lag between grant start and coverage effect.
- **Model 3** (accelerating introduction AND improving rollout quality — e.g. RTS,S where the program is new but quality of implementation also matters): coverage today = 0%; speed-up of introduction and speed-up of steady state may differ from each other.
Flag as Medium/H if the model structure is inconsistent with the intervention type (e.g., coverage today = 0% for a program already running at 25% coverage, or no implementation lag for Model 2).

**TA grants — discount rate by benefit stream**: TA models should use **1.4%** for death-averting benefit streams (the temporal uncertainty component of GiveWell's discount rate) and **4%** for income/consumption benefit streams. Programs with mixed benefits (e.g., IFA with both morbidity and cognitive/income effects) should apply different rates to each stream. Flag as Medium/H if: (a) a death-averting TA model uses 4% (overstates discounting); (b) an income stream uses 1.4% (understates discounting); (c) a mixed model applies a single blended rate without documentation.

**TA grants — probability of failure**: The TA BOTEC should include an explicit "probability of failure" parameter — the probability that the TA engagement fails to shift the status quo (government doesn't adopt or doesn't change coverage trajectory). The default is ~30%. Flag as Medium/H if: (a) no p(failure) parameter exists — CE will be overstated without it; (b) p(failure) is below 15% without documented rationale (e.g., government has already publicly committed to the program and co-funded it); (c) p(failure) is above 50% — at this level the grant's expected value may be too low to clear the bar, and the researcher should document why it remains fundable.

**TA grants — speed-up parameter calibration**: The "speed-up of steady state due to TA" parameter is the most consequential and most speculative input in most TA BOTECs. Cross-check against GiveWell's program benchmarks: RTS,S ~2 years, DtW ~4 years, IFA ~4 years, Dual tests ~5 years, HPV ~6 years, ILC ~13 years. A speed-up significantly above the nearest benchmark without documented rationale is a flag. Also verify the implied year-on-year coverage growth rate is internally consistent with the speed-up parameter and the gap between coverage today and steady state.

**TA grants — counterfactual coverage trajectory**: The counterfactual (coverage without TA) is the most important structural assumption in a TA model. Flag as Medium/H if: (a) the counterfactual is modeled as completely stagnant (zero coverage growth) without a note explaining why the program would not progress at all without TA — this is a strong assumption that requires justification (e.g., budget frozen, government capacity constraint, no competing funder); (b) the counterfactual curve is steeper than comparable programs without documentation; (c) the model does not make explicit predictions about how coverage should change, making the model unverifiable against future M&E data.

**TA grants — rollout lag for coverage-improvement models (Model 2)**: For Model 2 TA BOTECs (improving coverage of an existing program), verify the benefit stream does not begin in Year 1 without an explicit implementation lag. TA typically requires a ramp-up period: government needs time to absorb the TA outputs, update procurement, train staff, and see coverage changes appear in data. A benefit stream starting at full effect in Year 1 implies the TA produced measurable coverage change within the first program year, which is uncommon for capacity-building TA. Flag as **Medium/H** if: (a) the coverage benefit ramp begins in Year 1 without a note explaining why immediate effect is expected; (b) no "year TA begins to increase coverage" parameter is present (or is set to Year 1) for a program that does not have a pre-existing fast-action mechanism (e.g., a single government ministerial decision that could take effect quickly). Recommended fix: add a note explaining the assumed lag, or set the benefit start year to at least Year 2 with a rationale. Exception: if the TA involves delivering a commodity or issuing a budget authorization that can take effect quickly (e.g., securing emergency malaria drug procurement), a Year 1 start may be defensible — confirm this is documented.

**TA grants — supplemental adjustments: additive vs. compounding**: When a TA BOTEC applies multiple supplemental adjustments to the initial CE estimate (e.g., "+7% folic acid benefit," "+2% neonatal mortality benefit," "−15% screen-and-treat overlap"), read the formula or structure combining them. Check whether they are summed additively or compounded multiplicatively, then ask: do these adjustments affect independent mechanisms, or do they share a common pathway (e.g., multiple adjustments all flowing through iron metabolism, or multiple adjustments affecting the same population subgroup)?

- **Independent adjustments** (e.g., folic acid benefit vs. leverage discount — unrelated mechanisms): additive is acceptable.
- **Correlated / same-pathway adjustments** (e.g., two adjustments both flowing through iron/folic acid bioavailability): multiplicative compounding is more technically correct. Additive summation of correlated adjustments can overstate the net effect.

File as **Low/H** with Researcher judgment needed ✓ if two or more adjustments share a plausible common mechanism and are combined additively: "[cells]: supplemental adjustments [A = +X%, B = +Y%] are summed additively (net = [X+Y]%). If these share a common mechanism, multiplicative compounding gives [X×Y result]% instead. Confirm additive treatment is intentional and document the independence assumption." Do not file if: (a) all adjustment magnitudes are small (<5% each) and the additive vs. multiplicative difference is trivial (<1%); or (b) a cell note already explains why additive combination is appropriate.
